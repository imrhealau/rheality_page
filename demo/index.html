Reading a disaster before it happened

On 25 January 2019, the Dam I tailings dam at Corrego do Feijao in Brumadinho, Brazil collapsed without public warning, releasing about 12 million cubic metres of mine waste and killing 270 people. In the year before it fell, the dam face was already moving, and that movement was measurable from space.

This is Rheality's monitoring dashboard, replayed over the 12 months before the collapse using free Sentinel-1 satellite radar. It shows what an operator, regulator or insurer would have seen: slow creep turning into anomalous acceleration, and an alert raised 40 days before the wall gave way. No ground sensors, no site visit, one independent read from orbit.

    Beyond this dam

    Brumadinho is a tailings dam, but the same monitoring engine watches any ground that can move: slopes and landslides, reclaimed land and runways, ports and rail, and the data centres and LNG terminals going up across Asia now. The AI build-out is putting billions of dollars of data centres onto fast-reclaimed land in Johor, Batam and Jakarta; the insurers and lenders financing them, and the contractors building on still-settling ground, need independent proof the site is stable from the reclamation phase onward.

    Demo note. This dashboard is Rheality's monitoring front-end. The displacement series shown is reconstructed from the published post-event analysis of Dam I (Grebby et al. 2021, Communications Earth & Environment), which found sub-36 mm/yr face creep accelerating anomalously about 40 days before the collapse. In production the same panels read a displacement table generated by our own InSAR pipeline from free Sentinel-1 radar. It demonstrates the product and the alerting logic, not an original processing result.
